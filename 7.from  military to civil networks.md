## Core Lecture Notes
### 1. Historical Context (The "Why")
*What was happening politically, socially, or economically right before this event?*
- **Cold War world (1945–1991):**  
    U.S. vs. Soviet Union. Huge fear of nuclear war and surprise attack. The U.S. government pours money into tech that can **survive disruption** and give better command, control, and intelligence.
    
- **1960s–1970s:**  
    ARPA (later DARPA) funds experiments in **packet switching** to keep communications working even if some sites are destroyed. That’s where **ARPANET** comes from: mainly military + elite universities doing research for defense.
    
- **1980s:**  
    The Cold War is still on, but computing is spreading into universities and science labs. The **NSF** uses the same ideas (TCP/IP, “network of networks”) to connect **civilian research** across the country (NSFNET). This is science, not just war.
    
- **Late 1980s–early 1990s:**  
    Cold War ends (Soviet Union collapses in 1991). The U.S. shifts from pure military rivalry to **economic and technological leadership**. The same open standards move into **commercial networks**, and private companies run the backbone. That’s the civilian Internet era.

### 2. Key Figures & Groups
*Double-bracket key figures (e.g., [[Abraham Lincoln]]) to link them.*
- - **ARPA / DARPA** (Advanced Research Projects Agency)
    
    - Defense Department agency funding high‑risk tech during the Cold War.
    - Bankrolled early packet‑switching research and ARPANET.
- **NSF (National Science Foundation)**
    
    - Civilian science agency.
    - Funded **NSFNET**, the academic backbone that helped push TCP/IP into the wider world.

### 3. Major Events & Timeline
*Note chronological developments.*
- **1960–1966 – Concepts, not yet a network**

- Paul **Baran** (RAND, U.S.) and Donald **Davies** (UK) develop **packet switching** ideas for resilient communications.

**1968–1972 – ARPANET is built**

- ARPA funds **ARPANET**.
- **1969:** first 4 nodes (UCLA, SRI, UCSB, Utah) linked.
- Early 1970s: ARPANET grows across U.S. research sites.

**1973–1983 – From one network to “network of networks”**

- **1973–74:** Vint **Cerf** and Bob **Kahn** design **TCP/IP**.
- Late 1970s: experimental TCP/IP networks tested.
- **Jan 1, 1983 (“flag day”)**: ARPANET officially **switches to TCP/IP** → technical birth of the **Internet** idea.

**Mid‑1980s – Civilian research backbone**

- **1985+**: **NSFNET** created to connect supercomputer centers and universities using TCP/IP.
- Internet = multiple networks (ARPANET, NSFNET, others) talking via TCP/IP.

**Late 1980s–early 1990s – Commercialization**

- Commercial **ISPs** appear; more non-academic networks join.
- **1990:** ARPANET is formally decommissioned.
- **1995:** NSFNET backbone shut down; commercial backbones fully take over → civilian/commercial Internet era.

### 4. Cause and Effect
*What sparked these events, and what were the consequences?*
- **Cause 2: Academic open culture + ARPA funding**  
→ **Mechanism:** researchers share ideas via **RFCs**, design **open standards** (TCP/IP), run code on university machines.  
→ **Effect:**

- Protocols are **public and interoperable**.
- Easy to connect new networks → “network of networks” Internet.

**Cause 3: End-to-end design choice** (smart edges, simple core)  
→ **Mechanism:** routers mostly just forward packets; apps and hosts handle logic and security.  
→ **Effects:**

- Super easy to innovate at the edge (new apps, devices).
- Security/privacy mostly an afterthought, done app‑by‑app, not deeply baked into the core.

**Cause 4: Shift to NSFNET, then commercial backbones**  
→ **Mechanism:** same open TCP/IP stack, but now run by NSF and then private ISPs under market incentives.  
→ **Effects:**

- Massive civilian and global growth.
- Network effects + data concentration → a few large platforms and backbone providers gain a lot of power.

## Key Terms & Concepts
- **Concept / Term 1:** Definition and significance.
- **Cold War problem:** needed a resilient way to share data across distant labs, even under attack → pushed funding into new network designs.
- **Packet switching:** chop data into packets that can take different routes → more resilient and efficient than old circuit-switched phone lines.
- **Open standards (TCP/IP) + RFCs:** military money + academic openness → shared, public “rules” anyone could implement → enabled a global **network of networks**.
- **End-to-end principle:** keep the core simple, push intelligence (and security) to the edges → huge innovation upside, but made strong, universal security and privacy harder to bolt on later.
- **Transition path:** ARPANET (Defense) → NSFNET (research/education) → commercial ISPs running the backbone → same open protocols, new for‑profit incentives, helping drive market concentration.

Th**Milgram’s Obedience Experiment (1961)**

- Goal: See **how far ordinary people would go in harming another person** when ordered by an authority figure.
    
- Roles:
    
    - **Experimenter** – authority (lab coat)
    - **Teacher** – real participant (presses “shocks”)
    - **Learner** – actor (pretends to be shocked)
- Procedure (simple flow):
    
    1. Teacher reads word pairs, tests Learner
    2. Wrong answer → Teacher gives a “shock”
    3. Shock level increases step by step (up to 450V)
    4. Learner protests, then goes silent
    5. Experimenter uses prompts:
        - “Please continue.”
        - “The experiment requires that you continue.”
- Key result:
    
    > About **65%** of participants went all the way to the **maximum shock (450V)** when the Experimenter insisted.
    

**Core Concepts**

- **Authority**
    
    > Person with **legitimate power** in a situation (e.g., boss, doctor, teacher, officer).
    
- **Obedience**
    
    > **Behavior change** in response to a **direct order** from an authority.
    
    - Example: boss says “Stay late and finish this.” → you stay.
- **Agentic State**
    
    > Mental state where a person sees themselves as an **agent for someone else’s orders**, not as acting on their own.
    
    - Feels like: “I’m just following orders; it’s not really my responsibility.”
    - Seen in Milgram: “I didn’t want to do it, but you told me I had to continue.”

**Situational Factors that Change Obedience**  
Use the mnemonic **P.L.A.P.**

1. **P – Proximity of Authority**
    
    - Authority **close / in the room** → **higher** obedience.
    - Authority giving orders by **phone** → obedience **drops**.
2. **L – Legitimacy / Prestige**
    
    - High-prestige setting (e.g., well-known university / official lab coat) → **more** obedience.
    - Less formal / lower-prestige setting → **less** obedience.
3. **A – Authority Presence**
    
    - Authority **physically present** and watching → people more likely to obey.
    - If they leave the room → people feel freer to disobey or cheat.
4. **P – Peer Dissent (or Support)**
    
    - Peers who **obey** → obedience stays **high**.
    - At least one peer who **refuses** and says “I won’t do this” → obedience **drops a lot**.
    - Peer dissent makes it easier to say “no” yourself.

**Everyday Applications (Examples)**

- **Workplace:** boss asks for risky or unethical actions → people may obey because of authority + job security.
    - Resistance: **stand with coworkers**, raise concerns together (peer dissent).
- **School:** students follow teacher’s instructions even if they feel it’s unfair.
- **Military / hierarchical organizations:** strong authority + culture of obedience can push people to follow orders that conflict with personal morals.

## 🧠 Main Terms & Concepts
* Change behavior: 
	* Stop or change the behavior causing the conflict.
* Change Belief/attitude: 
	* adjust beliefs so it fits my acions
* Add consonant cognitions(extra reasons):
	* keep belief and behavior, but pile on justifying reasons
* Trivialize / deny ("its not that big deal"):
	* Downplay the importance of the conflict or the behavior
*  **Cognitive dissonance** = the uncomfortable mental tension you feel when your actions, beliefs, values, or self-image don’t match — and you then mentally justify or adjust things to reduce that tension.
	* eg. “I care about my health” + “I smoke a lot” → tension → so you justify, change beliefs, or change behavior to feel better.
* Two types of dissonance:
	*   **Effort justification:** When we put allot of effort,pain, or embarrassment into somthing that turns out to be disappointing, we feel dissonance and convince ourselves it was worth it.
		* .e.g. very hard class that is boring => "This course is crucial for my future"
	* **Post-decision dissonance(buyers remorse):** Dissonance you feel after choosing between options, when you start thinking about the good things you gave up.

## 🔬 Studies & Experiments
### Study: [$1 vs. $20 study]
*   **Researcher(s):** **Festinger & Carlsmith (1959) 
*   **Purpose/Hypothesis:** Why we lie to ourselves
*   **Methodology:** 
	-    Participants did a **very boring task** for ~1 hour.
	-  Then they were asked to tell the next person: “The task was fun and interesting.”
- Payment conditions:
    - Group 1: **$1**
    - Group 2: **$20**
- Later, they reported how much they actually liked the task.
* 
* **Results:** 
	* -  **$1 group**: said they liked the task **more**.
	- **$20 group**: were more honest that it was boring.
	- **Explanation (cognitive dissonance):**

		- Everyone: “The task was boring” + “I told someone it was fun” → conflict.
		- $20 group: “I lied because I got paid a lot” → good external excuse → **low dissonance**.
		- $1 group: “I lied for almost nothing” → no good excuse → **high dissonance**.  
		    To reduce it, many **changed their attitude**: “Maybe it was kind of interesting.”
		    → Shows we sometimes change our **beliefs** to match our **actions**.
*   **Conclusions:** 
	* **Conclusion – Why We Lie to Ourselves**

Cognitive dissonance explains why we sometimes **edit our beliefs or stories** instead of facing uncomfortable truths about our actions. When what we _do_ clashes with what we _believe_ (or how we see ourselves), we feel mental tension. To reduce that tension, we usually: change our **behavior**, change our **beliefs**, **add justifying reasons**, or **downplay** how serious the conflict is.

The $1 vs $20 experiment showed that people can even change their **attitudes** to match their past behavior. Special cases like **effort justification** and **post-decision dissonance** (buyer’s remorse) show how powerful this drive for inner consistency is. In everyday life, this means we’re often not lying to others as much as we’re **quietly lying to ourselves** so we can keep seeing ourselves as good, smart, and consistent.

## **4. Why people do this (explanations)**

**Four main dissonance-reduction strategies**

When beliefs and behavior clash, we usually reduce dissonance by one (or more) of these:

1. **Change behavior**
    
    - Stop or change the behavior causing the conflict.
    - Example: “I value health” + “I smoke” → **quit smoking** or cut down.
2. **Change belief/attitude**
    
    - Adjust the belief so it fits your actions.
    - Example: “I smoke” → “Enjoying my life now is more important than long-term health.”
3. **Add consonant cognitions (add extra reasons)**
    
    - Keep belief and behavior, but **pile on justifying reasons**.
    - Example: “I smoke, but…”
        - “I exercise a lot.”
        - “It’s the only thing that calms me down.”
        - “I’ll quit later when life is less stressful.”
4. **Trivialize / deny (“it’s not a big deal”)**
    
    - Downplay the importance of the conflict or the behavior.
    - Example:
        - “Yeah, smoking is bad, but everything is bad for you.”
        - “I only smoke socially; it’s not a real problem.”

## 🔗 Related Notes
* Cognitive dissonance is **not** about being stupid or weak; it’s a **normal mental process** that everyone uses to protect their self-image.
*  The tension shows up most when something threatens how we see ourselves:  
    “I’m a good person / smart / healthy / loyal” vs evidence that doesn’t fit.
*  We usually notice the **story** (“it wasn’t that bad,” “I had no choice”) more than we notice the **dissonance** underneath it.
* Dissonance can be **useful** when we face it honestly: it can push us to **change behavior** (e.g., actually quit, apologize, improve) instead of just rationalizing.
*  Big red flag in life: when you catch yourself giving **lots of excuses** or saying “it doesn’t really matter” over and over, that’s often dissonance talking.

## 📝 Summary (Active Recall)
  
Cognitive dissonance is the uncomfortable tension we feel when our actions don’t match our beliefs or self-image. To reduce this tension, we usually change our behavior, change our beliefs, add extra justifying reasons, or say the conflict isn’t a big deal. The $1 vs $20 experiment showed people even changed their attitudes to match what they had already done.

## 📌 Overview
*   **Key Themes:** FIrst Impression bias

*   **Key Theorists/Researchers:** 
	* Nisbett and Wilson(1977)

---

## 🧠 Main Terms & Concepts
*   **Halo Effect:** when one positive thing about a person(like being attractive, friendly, or well spoken) makes us assume lots of other positive things about them(smart, funny, kind, competent), even without evidence.
	* So our brain instead of judgeing each trait seperatly, our brains lets one “bright” spot, light up the whole picture.🌟
*   **Horn Effect:** the opposite of the halo, one negative detail makes us assume lots of other negative traits(same bias just negative instead of positive).
* Halo Effect → one positive cue → we assume more positive
* Horn Effect → one negative cue → we assume more negative
* Both are first impression biases: we form a quick overall impression, and that impression colors how we see everthing else about the person.

* **First Impression Bias** - Our very first quick impression shapes how we see ad remember almost everything about a person.

---

## 🔬 Studies & Experiments
### Study: [Nisbett and Wilson’s Warm vs Cold study with a filmed instructor]
*   **Researcher(s):** Nisbett and Wilson (1977)
*   **Purpose/Hypothesis:** Warm v.s. Cold first impression
*   **Methodology:** 
	* Students saw a video of the same instructor
	* in one version, he acted warm and friendly
	* in another, he acted cold and distant
	* Then students rated his traits: how competent, how likable, how good his accent was, ect.
*   **Results:** 
	* They found that first impressions of whether someone dressed real nice people assume they were good at their job
	* and if they were dressed real poor the werent 
	* the opposite was usually the case
*   **Conclusions:** 
	* Avoid doing this by stopping to ask yourself what you actually know about the person not based off how they look

## **4. Why people do this (explanations)**

- What - **What they did (study set-up)**

- College students watched a video of **the same instructor**.
    
- Researchers changed only how he **behaved**:
    
    - In one version: he acted **warm and friendly**.
    - In another: he acted **cold and distant**.
- After the video, students rated him on several traits:
    
    - How **likeable** he was
    - How **competent/intelligent** he seemed
    - Even things like how **pleasant his accent** was
- In experiment terms:
    
    - **Independent Variable (IV):** warm/friendly vs. cold/distant behavior
    - **Dependent Variables (DVs):** students’ ratings of his traits (likeable, smart, accent, etc.
    
- Key distinctions (e.g., A vs. B):
- Students who saw the **warm** version rated him:
    - More likeable
    - More competent
    - Even judged his **accent** more positively
- Students who saw the **cold** version rated him:
    - Less likeable
    - Less competent
    - Judged his **accent** more negatively
- Important: the **content** of what he said (and his actual accent) did _not_ change — only the **warm vs. cold** behavior.
- What they found -
	- **What it means (implications)**

- A **first impression** (warm vs. cold) can create a **halo or horn effect**:
    - One early cue spreads to many other judgments.
- People are often **not aware** that this is happening; they think they’re judging each trait separately.
- This matters in:
    - **Hiring/interviews** (how we read “professionalism” or “competence”)
    - **Teaching** (how students rate instructors)
    - **Everyday social life** (how we judge coworkers, dates, strangers)

## 📝 Summary (Active Recall)
The **halo effect** is a bias where one positive trait (like warmth or attractiveness) makes us assume a bunch of other positive traits. The **horn effect** is the negative version. In **Nisbett & Wilson’s 1977 study**, students watched a video of the _same_ instructor acting either **warm/friendly** or **cold/distant**. That one difference (the IV) made students rate many other traits (the DVs) more positively or negatively, even things like his accent that didn’t actually change.

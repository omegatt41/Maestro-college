## 🧠 Main Terms & Concepts:

* **Prospect Theory:** People evaluate outcomes as gains or losses relative to a reference point, not in absolute terms.
* **Reference Point:** Your mental “Status quo” or expectations (e.g. what you thought youd get or lose)

* **
* S shaped value function:
* **Reference Dependent** - Curve crosses zero at the reference point(no gain/loss there)
* **Concave for gains** - (above the reference point):
	* Each extra gain feels less special than the last (dimished sesitivity)
* **Convex for Loss** -(Below the reference point):
	* Each extra loss hurst but the last huts the most
	* The curve is steeper for losses than it is for gains
**Loss Aversion**:
	A loss of $X hurts more than a gain of $X
**Risk Preferences** - Gains vs losses
	In the gain domain things framed as wins/saves:
		- people are typically risk-averse
		- Prefer sure smaller gains than risky large gains
	In the loss domain things framed as loss/deaths:
		People are often risk seeking
		prefer a riskier option over a sure loss
Framing and prospect theory:
	Same stats diff wording - dif choices
		Gain frame - lives saved:

- **Gain frame (“lives saved”)**
    - Program A: 200 will be saved for sure.
    - Program B: 1/3 chance 600 saved, 2/3 chance 0 saved.
    - Most people choose **A** → **risk-averse** in gains. (You chose A ✅)
- **Loss frame (“people will die”)**
    
    - Program C: 400 will die for sure.
    - Program D: 1/3 chance nobody dies, 2/3 chance 600 die.
    - Most people choose **D** → **risk-seeking** in losses.



## **4. Everyday Explanations**
- **Insurance & warranties**
    - People overpay to avoid a _possible loss_ (phone breaking, car issue).
- **Investing / gambling**
    - “Chasing losses” to get back to even → risk-seeking in the loss domain.
- **Marketing**
    - “Save $50” vs. “Avoid a $50 fee” → the _loss-framed_ version usually motivates more

## 📝 Summary (Active Recall)
#review
- **Prospect theory**: People evaluate outcomes as **gains or losses relative to a reference point**, not in absolute terms.
- **Reference point**: Your mental “status quo” or expectation (e.g., what you thought you’d get or lose).
- **S-shaped value function**:
    - **Concave for gains** → diminishing sensitivity to larger gains.
    - **Convex for losses** → similar pattern for losses, but…
    - **Steeper for losses** → losses hurt more than equal gains feel good.
- **Loss aversion**: Losing $X feels worse than gaining $X feels good.
- **Risk behavior**:
    - **Gains** → people are usually **risk-averse** (prefer sure smaller gain).
    - **Losses** → people become **risk-seeking** (willing to gamble to avoid sure loss).
- **Framing** (lives saved vs. lives lost) can flip choices, even when the underlying numbers are the same, because it shifts whether we see a **gain** or a **loss**.

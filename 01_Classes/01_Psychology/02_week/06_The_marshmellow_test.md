**1. The Marshmallow Test – What it is**

- A child sits alone with **1 treat now** (like a marshmallow).
- Adult says:
    
    > “If you **wait** and don’t eat this until I come back, you’ll get **2 treats**.”
    
- The child must choose:
    - **Smaller-now reward**: eat 1 treat immediately
    - **Larger-later reward**: wait and get 2 treats
- Researchers watch:
    - Who waits, who doesn’t
    - What tricks/strategies kids use to stop themselves

**2. Delay of Gratification**

> **Delay of gratification** = choosing a **larger, later reward** over a **smaller, immediate reward**.

Examples:

- Marshmallow: 1 now vs 2 later
- Recovery: quick high now vs long-term health, freedom, better life
- Phone at night: scrolling now vs better sleep and energy tomorrow

**3. Self-Control and Willpower**

- **Self-control**
    
    > The ability to **override an impulse** so you can stick to a goal.
    
    - “I want recovery more than a high.”
    - “I won’t check my phone; I’ll go to sleep.”
    - “I’ll study before I game.”
- **Willpower**
    
    > The **mental energy / drive** that powers acts of self-control in the moment.
    
    - The “push” you feel when you **actually do** the harder, better thing.

Think:

- Self-control = the **skill/decision**
- Willpower = the **fuel/mindset** that lets you follow through

Your example:

- Impulse: use drugs
- Goal: real recovery
- Self-control: choosing extra meetings instead of using
- Willpower: the inner strength that helped you make and keep that choice

**4. Strategies to Help Self-Control (from the marshmallow test)**

1. **Attentional control (focus)**
    
    > Change what you look at or focus on.
    
    - Look away from the marshmallow
    - Stare at the ceiling, sing, play with the chair
    - Idea: “If I stop staring at it, it’s easier not to eat it.”
2. **Cognitive reframing (meaning)**
    
    > Change how you **think about** the tempting thing.
    
    - “This marshmallow is just a picture, not real food.”
    - “This high is actually a trap, not a reward.”
    - “This scroll is stealing energy from tomorrow.”
3. **Situation modification (environment)**
    
    > Change the **physical setup** to make giving in harder.
    
    - Push the marshmallow to the far edge of the table
    - Sit on your hands so you can’t grab it
    - **Your example**: leave your phone in the car and use an alarm clock instead
    - Also: don’t keep certain substances or triggers anywhere near you

Key idea: smart strategies **support** self-control so you’re not relying only on “inner strength.”

**5. Real-Life “Marshmallow Tests”**

- **Substances / Recovery**
    
    - Small-now: use and feel good briefly
    - Big-later: sobriety, health, trust, freedom
    - Strategies: meetings, support people, avoiding certain places, changing routines
- **Phone at night**
    
    - Small-now: dopamine from scrolling
    - Big-later: better rest, mood, and focus next day
    - Strategy you designed:
        - Situation modification → leave phone in the car, use a separate alarm clock
- **Studying / Work**
    
    - Small-now: TV, games, snacks, chatting
    - Big-later: passing class, job options, pride in performance
    - Strategies: work in a quiet place, keep distractions in another room, set timers

**6. Long-Term Outcomes (High-Level)**

- In follow-ups, kids who waited longer in the marshmallow test tended, on average, to show:
    - Better **school/academic** outcomes
    - Better **social/relationship** outcomes
- Idea: early **self-control skills** help people:
    - Finish boring but important tasks
    - Save money
    - Pause before reacting
    - Stick with long-term goals

Not about “good kids vs bad kids” — it’s about:

- Skills (self-control strategies)
- Situations (how tempting the environment is)
- And how those two work together over time

## 🧠 Main Terms & Concepts
* **Reconstructive memory in your own words (your idea, polished)**

- The brain stores a **rough template** of events, not a full recording.
- When you recall, it **fills in the blanks** with:
    - what’s most probable
    - what you’ve been told
    - what you expect
- That filling-in can make memories:
    - **misleading** (distorted)
    - or **completely false**
*  **A False Memory** A memory that feels real and detailed but is wrong or partially made up (“like remembering broken glass that never existed)
*   **A Leading question** -
    
    > A question that **suggests** a certain answer or makes an event sound more intense/definite than it really was.
    
- Example:
    
    - Neutral: “How fast were the cars going when they **hit** each other?”
    - Leading: “How fast were the cars going when they **smashed into** each other?”
- Effect:
    
    - Stronger wording → more intense mental image →  
        higher speed estimates + more chance of **false details** (like broken glass).

## 🔬 Studies & Experiments
### Study: [Loftus and Palmer’s Car Crash Study(1974)]
*   **Researcher(s):** Loftus and Palmer
*   **Purpose/Hypothesis:** 
*   **Methodology:** 
	**Loftus & Palmer (1974) – Car-crash study**

1. **Setup**
    
    - Participants watched a **video of a car accident**.
    - Later, they were asked:
        
        > “About how fast were the cars going when they ___ each other?”
        
    - Different groups got different verbs:
        - **smashed**
        - hit
        - bumped
        - collided
        - contacted
*   **Results:** – **Speed estimates**
    
    - **“Smashed” → highest speed estimates**
    - Milder verbs (“hit,” “contacted”) → lower speed estimates
    - Same video, different **wording** → different **memory-based judgments**.
2. **One week later – Broken glass question**
    
    - They were asked: “Did you see **any broken glass**?” (Yes/No)
    - Fact: **There was NO broken glass** in the film.
    - Those who heard **“smashed”** earlier were:
        - **More likely** to say “Yes, I saw broken glass.”
    - Shows that wording not only changed **estimates**, but also changed what people **“remembered” seeing.**

## **4. Why people do this (explanations)**
**Real-world impact (eyewitnesses)**

- Eyewitness memory can be changed by:
    - police questions (“Did you see **the** gun?”)
    - media coverage
    - talking with others
- Because memory is reconstructive:
    - People can confidently testify about things that **never happened**.
    - So courts and police need to be careful with **leading questions**.

## 📝 Summary (Active Recall)
**1. Memory is reconstructive**

- Your brain doesn’t replay a video.
- It **rebuilds** memories each time from pieces: real details + expectations + things heard later.

**2. False memories**

- You can have **vivid, confident memories** of things that didn’t happen (or didn’t happen that way).
- Example: “Remembering” broken glass in the car-crash video when there was none.

**3. Loftus & Palmer (1974) – car-crash study**

- People watched the **same crash video**.
- Question wording changed: “How fast were the cars going when they **smashed/hit/bumped/contacted** each other?”
- **“Smashed” → highest speed estimates** and more people later “remembered” **broken glass** that wasn’t there.

**4. Leading questions**

- A **leading question** nudges you toward a certain answer or a more intense picture.
- Strong words like **“smashed”** can change how you **reconstruct** the event in memory.

**5. Real-world impact**

- Eyewitnesses can **honestly** report details that are wrong.
- Police and courts must be careful with **question wording**, because it can shape memory.

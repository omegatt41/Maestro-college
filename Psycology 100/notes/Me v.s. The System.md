## 📝 Summary (Active Recall)
**Core idea: Me vs. the system**

- **B = f(P, S)**
    
    > Behavior = function of **Person (P)** and **Situation (S)**
    
    - **P:** values, personality, history, moral beliefs
    - **S:** authority, group norms, roles, rules, consequences
- You are **never just “you”** and never just “a victim of the situation” — both push on behavior.

**Milgram (obedience to authority)**

- People obey harmful orders more when:
    - Authority seems **legitimate/prestigious**
    - Authority is **physically/psychologically close**
    - There are **no models of disobedience**
    - They feel **less personal responsibility** (“just following orders”)
- Resistance grows when:
    - You feel **personally responsible**
    - Authority seems **less legitimate**
    - You see or become a **disobedient model**

Applied to you (sales scenario): manager + company = authority; you resist by **owning responsibility** and refusing.

**Asch (conformity to group)**

- People conform more when:
    - The group is **unanimous**
    - They must respond **publicly**
    - They fear **rejection or conflict**
- Conformity drops when:
    - Even **one ally** disagrees
    - You can respond **privately**
    - Norms say it’s okay to **speak up**

Applied to you: the **group** is your biggest pressure point, but even **one coworker dissenting** would help you resist.

**Zimbardo (roles & the power of the situation)**

- Roles + setting can pull people into behavior that **violates their usual values**.
- Key insight: you can **redefine your role** instead of letting the role define you.
    - Example: “I’m not a pressure-salesperson; I’m an **honest advisor** to customers.”

Applied to you: you chose Zimbardo as key — you’d resist more if you define your role as **protecting customers**, not exploiting them.

**Moral courage**

> Willingness to act against group/authority pressure because it violates your morals, even at a cost.

- Can be:
    - **Quiet resistance:** not doing what you’re told
    - **Active resistance:** saying, “This isn’t right; I won’t do it.”
- Your statement: you’d **stand up**, tell your work you won’t force sales because it violates your values → clear **moral courage**.

**Your personal takeaway**

- You see yourself as:
    - **Mostly shaped by your values (P)**
    - Still aware that **situation (S)** — especially the group — creates pressure
- Your “if–then” anti‑conformity plan (sales example):
    - _If_ work pressures me to treat customers like cash cows,
    - _Then_ I will **refuse and say it’s against my values and not fair to customers.**

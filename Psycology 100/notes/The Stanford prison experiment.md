## 🔬 Studies & Experiments
### Study: [The stanford prison experiment(1971)]
*   **Researcher(s):** Phillip Zimbardo
*   **Purpose/Hypothesis:**
	* i f you put _normal, healthy people_ into a **simulated prison** and randomly assign them to be **guards** or **prisoners**,  
		then the **prison environment and roles** will be powerful enough to change their behavior — sometimes in extreme ways. 
	* Aim to study how people conform to social roles(guards vs prisoners)
*   **Methodology:** **Can an ordinary situation + role push ordinary people into cruel or submissive behavior?**
* **Duration:** 2 weeks
* **Procedure:** 24 male college students screened for mental and physical health were randomly selected to be guards or prisoners
	* Prisoners were arrested at home(surprise), given uniforms and ID numbers
		* they were referred to only by there number(loss of identity)
	* Guards were;
		* given  uniforms, Sunglasses, batons
		* Told to maintian order(no specific taining)
*   **Results:** 
	* Guards became:
		* Abusive, controlling and abusive
	* Prisoners became:
		* Passive anxious, depressed , some prisoners had emotional breakdowns
		* Experiment stopped early due to ethical reasons
*   **Conclusions:** 
		*  Psychological harm to participants
		* Lack of truly informed consent
		* Participants couldnt easily withdraw
		* Zimbardo played dual role(researcher + prison superintendent)
		* Demand characteritics people acted how to thought they should
		* some argue behavior was exaggerated or influenced by researchers

## 📝 Summary (Active Recall)
Ordinary people can act in extreme ways when placed in powerful or powerless roles due to situational pressures
Think of the Stanford Prison Experiment as **R.I.P.E.** — the situation was _ripe_ for bad behavior:

- **R – Role labels**: “Guard” vs. “Prisoner” told people _who_ they were supposed to be.
- **I – Institutional setting**: A fake **prison** in a university basement felt like a real institution with rules.
- **P – Power asymmetry**: Guards had **power**, prisoners had almost none.
- **E – Emotional distance**: Uniforms, sunglasses, ID numbers → easier to see others as “objects,” not people.

All four together made it much easier for guards to **morally disengage** and say (like you put it),  
“it’s the uniform/role, not really me.”

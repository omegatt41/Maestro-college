# 2026-03-31 - The Bystander Effect

## 📌 Overview
*   **Key Themes:** **Bystander effect** = when people are _less likely to help_ a person in trouble **when other people are present** than when they’re alone.
*   **Relevant Biological Processes:** In reality, things like **emotional arousal, stress responses, and empathy-related brain networks** are almost certainly involved when we see someone in trouble—but those biological processes are outside the scope of this unit
*   **Key Theorists/Researchers:** (1968)
	* **John M. Darley**
	* ** Bibb Latané **

---

## 🧠 Main Terms & Concepts
*   **Bystander effect:** - People are _less likely_ and _slower_ to help someone in trouble **when other people are present** than when they are alone.
*   **Diffusion of responsibility** - When more people are around, the _feeling of responsibility_ to help is **spread out** across everyone, so each individual feels **less personally responsible** and is less likely to act.

---

## 🔬 Studies & Experiments
### Study: [**Bystander Experiment**]
*   **Researcher(s):** Darley & Latané (1968) 
*   **Purpose/Hypothesis:** To test how the number of people around the incident as number of people around the slower the response times or no help at all
*   **Methodology:** 
- Setup:
    
    - College students in separate rooms, talking over an **intercom**.
    - They thought they were:
        - **Alone** with one other person
        - In a **small group**
        - In a **larger group**
    - During the discussion, one “participant” (a **recording**) seemed to have a **seizure** and called for help.
- What researchers measured:
    
    - **Whether** the participant went to get help.
    - **How long** it took them to act.
*   **Results:** - Main findings (pattern):
    
    - **Alone** → most people helped, and they helped **quickly**.
    - **Small group** → fewer helped, and they took **longer**.
    - **Larger group** → the **fewest** helped, and they were **slowest**.
    - So: **as group size increased, helping decreased and slowed down.**
*   **Conclusions:** **Why the number of people matters**

- When **alone**:
    
    - Thoughts like: “If I don’t help, no one will.”
    - Responsibility feels **high and personal** → more action.
- In a **crowd**:
    
    - Thoughts like: “Other people see this too.” / “Someone else will do something.”
    - Responsibility feels **shared** → **diffused**.
    - Each person feels **less pressure** to act → more waiting and inaction.
- This is a **situational force**, not just about personality:
    
    - Same person can act **very differently** when alone vs. in a group.
    - **Real-life examples**

- A customer drops items in a store:
    
    - Many shoppers walk by, expecting **someone else** (or staff) to help.
    - That’s diffusion of responsibility in everyday life.
- Busy street vs. quiet side street:
    
    - Quiet street, you’re the only one → more likely to step in.
    - Busy area with lots of people → easier to hesitate and assume others will act.

## 🔗 Related Notes
*   In **1964**, a woman named **Kitty Genovese** was murdered outside her apartment building. Early news reports (later found to be exaggerated) claimed that **many neighbors saw or heard parts of the attack but didn’t help or call police**.

That raised a disturbing question for Darley and Latané:

> “How can so many people witness an emergency and _no one_ step in?”

Instead of just guessing, they designed controlled experiments (like the seizure-intercom study) to test:

- how **group size** affects helping
- how **responsibility** shifts when others are present

So the Kitty Genovese case was the key real-world event that inspired their research into the **bystander effect** and **diffusion of responsibility**.

## Key Terms & Concepts
- **Concept / Term 1:** Definition and significance.
-  **Deterence:** Preventing an enemy attack by making the cost of war too high
- **Escalation:** When a conflict increases in intensity and becomes harder to control
- **Retalliation:** Striking back after being striked
- **Miscalculation:** When leader misjudge an opponents actions or intentions
- **Militarization:** Building permanant readiness through  weapons, bases, planning,spending, and defense institutions
- **Alliance Structure:** A long term security partnership between nations

## Summary
*Synthesize the overarching takeaway of this lecture in 2-3 sentences.*
### What deterrence reduced

Deterrence reduced the chance of direct invasion or open war between major powers because the consequences could be devastating.

### What deterrence increased

Deterrence increased the danger that a crisis could spiral out of control.

A small confrontation could become catastrophic if one side felt forced to act quickly or retaliate.

Simple cause-and-effect chain

**Nuclear weapons exist**  
→ direct war becomes extremely risky  
→ both sides try to deter each other  
→ crises become tense because signals can be misread  
→ leaders build permanent military readiness  
→ alliances and militarization become long-term Cold War structures

Early Cold War flashpoints

### Berlin Crisis / Berlin Airlift

The Soviet Union pressured West Berlin.  
The U.S. and its allies responded by supplying West Berlin.

**Long-term effect:**  
It strengthened Western cooperation and helped harden alliance thinking.

### Korean War

North Korea invaded South Korea.  
The U.S. viewed the conflict through Cold War containment logic.

**Long-term effect:**  
It convinced U.S. leaders that communist expansion could be global and coordinated, requiring military readiness and collective security.

Why flashpoints mattered

Crises like Berlin and Korea made U.S. leaders believe that weakness could invite aggression.

So instead of treating each crisis as temporary, the U.S. moved toward:

- permanent military readiness
- larger defense spending
- stronger alliances
- containment strategy
- faster response planning
